## Key Findings

### Conversations / Dialogue behavior
- Replies are direct, accusatory, and rarely deferential.
- They challenge the person as much as the argument: "How is it 'difficult'?", "Maybe you should add it."
- They use sarcasm and rhetorical escalation: "Sarcasm? You think this is a joke?"
- They respond to warnings with defiance or mockery.
- They can be verbally aggressive but usually stay within the bounds of recognizable argument.

### Decisions / Action patterns
- Decision criterion: whose side is closer to truth / less biased.
- They revert quickly and explain later, if at all.
- They escalate to noticeboards, arbitration, and blocks as instruments of war.
- They are willing to accept sanctions if they believe the cause is just.
- They rarely compromise unless forced by procedure.

## Patterns and Repeated Themes
- "You just need to quit pretending like NPOV was ever a part of Wikipedia."
- Reframing civility warnings as censorship.
- Treating every dispute as a test of intellectual honesty.
- Quick escalation and long grudges.

## Contradictions
- Anti-establishment posture while relying on Wikipedia's administrative machinery.
- Claims of truth-seeking while dismissing inconvenient sources.

## Inferences
- This archetype believes that politeness obscures power and that bluntness is honesty.
- They make decisions by identifying enemies of truth and opposing them.

## Gaps and Missing Information
- Limited evidence of off-wiki context (stress, group affiliation, ideology).
- No data on conversion rates to less confrontational styles.

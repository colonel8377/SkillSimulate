## Key Findings

### Writings / Substantive contributions
- Contributions are adversarial and framed as battles over truth vs. bias.
- Members write manifesto-like posts listing grievances, often accusing the project of hypocrisy in how it labels similar events differently depending on the group involved.
- They directly accuse other editors of bad faith, POV pushing, and ignorance.
- They invoke policy but as weapons rather than shared grammar: "intentionally ignoring them is a failure of good faith editing."

### Timeline / Cognitive trajectory
- Early phase: direct, blunt entry into disputes; little socialization.
- Mid phase: repeated conflict across multiple articles; accumulating warnings and blocks.
- Later phase: either entrenched warrior or disillusioned exit; some evolve toward procedural expertise as a new weapon.
- The trajectory is one of escalating confrontation rather than gradual integration.

## Patterns and Repeated Themes
- "You"-centered accusations.
- Lists of hypocrisies or double standards.
- Sarcasm and rhetorical questions.
- Rejection of civility norms as fake politeness.
- Belief that Wikipedia is a battlefield between truth and bias.

## Contradictions
- Claims to defend NPOV while using highly charged language.
- Demands good faith from others while assuming bad faith.

## Inferences
- This archetype sees conflict as the primary mechanism for producing truth.
- They derive identity from being the one willing to say what others avoid.

## Gaps and Missing Information
- No external psychological or ethnographic research on this cluster.
- Limited data on whether confrontation is strategic or temperamental.

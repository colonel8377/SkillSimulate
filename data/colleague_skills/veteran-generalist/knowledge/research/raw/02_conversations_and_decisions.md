## Key Findings

### Conversations / Dialogue behavior
- Replies vary dramatically in tone: friendly mentoring, blunt warnings, detailed reviews, dry bureaucratic notes.
- They are highly responsive and often reply quickly to many interlocutors.
- They use templates and policy shortcuts as conversational currency.
- They can be warm: "Welcome to Wikipedia!" and hostile: "Please stop vandalizing pages or you will be blocked."
- They thread deeply in disputes but also fire off many short, transactional messages.

### Decisions / Action patterns
- Decisions are fast and policy-informed: revert, warn, protect, delete, review.
- They rely on established procedures (AIV, RFPP, AfD, RfA, GA review) and route issues to the right venue.
- They make thousands of small decisions daily; correctness is assessed by policy fit, not personal preference.
- They block, protect pages, and nominate for deletion when thresholds are met.
- They mentor selectively, investing time in editors who show promise.

## Patterns and Repeated Themes
- "This is your last warning" as a routine tool.
- "Thanks" and barnstars mixed with sanctions.
- Quick triage: vandalism → revert; dispute → talk; bad article → AfD.
- High comfort with administrative language and templates.

## Contradictions
- Bureaucratic neutrality vs. personal hostility in some exchanges.
- Encourages participation while issuing blocks and warnings.
- Generalist identity vs. occasional fierce niche opinions.

## Inferences
- This archetype operates as the immune system and connective tissue of Wikipedia.
- They decide by matching situations to established procedures and community precedents.

## Gaps and Missing Information
- Limited data on emotional labor and burnout.
- No external study of how veteran generalists shape article quality over time.

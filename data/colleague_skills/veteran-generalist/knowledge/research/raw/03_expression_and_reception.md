## Key Findings

### Expression DNA
- Tone: highly variable — bureaucratic, friendly, sarcastic, hostile, mentorly.
- Cadence: bursty; long when explaining policy, short when patrolling or warning.
- Compression level: medium-to-high among peers; lower when mentoring.
- Certainty markers: confident, often procedural; uses "should," "will be blocked," "per policy."
- Signature moves:
  - Template-based warnings with customized addenda.
  - "Cheers," "Thanks," "Done" as quick social glue.
  - Mix of informal chat and formal administrative language.
  - Policy acronyms used as punctuation.
- Humor style: dry, sometimes playful with known peers.

### External views
- Likely seen by newcomers as intimidating or incomprehensible.
- Likely seen by peers as essential, reliable, and sometimes overbearing.
- Likely seen by rule-breakers as hostile enforcers.
- Rejected-behavior evidence includes some highly toxic outbursts, though these may be situational.
- Outside image: "the Wikipedia old-timer who knows where everything is and isn't afraid to block you."

## Patterns and Repeated Themes
- Institutional memory: references to past cases, precedents, and older editors.
- Social network maintenance: greetings, thanks, barnstars, inside jokes.
- Enforcement as community service.
- Adaptability across topics and namespaces.

## Contradictions
- Warmly social with peers; coldly procedural with strangers.
- Anti-bureaucratic rhetoric sometimes used by highly bureaucratic actors.
- Believes in openness while protecting pages and blocking users.

## Inferences
- Blind-test recognition would come from a mix of templates, policy acronyms, social niceties, and sudden administrative threats.

## Gaps and Missing Information
- No external ethnography of veteran generalist culture.
- Limited evidence on how this archetype's tone shifts between public and private spaces.

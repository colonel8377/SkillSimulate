## Key Findings

### Writings / Substantive contributions
- Contributions focus on identifying factual errors, contradictions, and sourcing problems.
- Members post concise corrections: "The family section contains info about Katie Kuster's college basketball career. The original entry... is no longer current."
- They flag internal inconsistencies: "In the intro it says BPRD is an international non-government entity but later it says it's a federal agency."
- They question claims and request sources without much social framing.
- They often edit in areas where they have domain knowledge or can quickly verify facts.

### Timeline / Cognitive trajectory
- Early phase: careful observer, points out errors, asks for sources.
- Mid phase: builds reputation as a reliable fact-checker; may specialize in a few domains.
- Later phase: either becomes a trusted reviewer or drifts away due to frustration with unsourced persistence.
- The trajectory is from error-spotter → domain checker → sometimes disillusioned verifier.

## Patterns and Repeated Themes
- Correction as the default move.
- Internal consistency checks.
- Requests for written/broadcast sources.
- Removal of unsourced or speculative content.
- Terse, factual prose with minimal hedging.

## Contradictions
- Values accuracy but can come across as pedantic or dismissive.
- Wants collaboration but often works alone to verify facts.
- Believes in sources yet sometimes relies on quick knowledge.

## Inferences
- This archetype treats Wikipedia as an accuracy project; social harmony is secondary to getting facts right.
- They derive satisfaction from catching errors that others miss.

## Gaps and Missing Information
- No external research on fact-checker motivations or expertise sources.
- Limited data on how they handle situations where sources conflict.

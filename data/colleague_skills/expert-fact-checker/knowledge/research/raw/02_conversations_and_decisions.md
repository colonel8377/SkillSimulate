## Key Findings

### Conversations / Dialogue behavior
- Replies are terse and evidence-centered.
- They ask direct factual questions: "Is that true?", "Can you provide a source?"
- They point out contradictions with minimal editorializing.
- They are less likely to engage in long policy debates; prefer to settle matters with sources.
- They can be blunt when dismissing poor sources: "It sounds like nonsense."

### Decisions / Action patterns
- Decision criterion: is the claim verifiable and correctly sourced?
- They remove or flag unsourced material quickly.
- They revert when facts are wrong and explain with a better source.
- They are cautious about accepting claims that rely on "I heard" or "everyone knows."
- They may disengage from debates where evidence is not forthcoming.

## Patterns and Repeated Themes
- "Source?" / "Citation?" / "Please provide a source."
- "This is incorrect" as a direct opener.
- Removal of speculation, trivia, and unsourced assertions.
- Preference for authoritative sources: official sites, books, broadcasts.

## Contradictions
- Demands rigor from others but occasionally makes quick corrections based on memory.
- Socially detached yet cares deeply about content quality.
- Trusts institutions and official sources while skeptical of crowdsourced claims.

## Inferences
- This archetype decides by matching claims to verifiable evidence.
- They are more interested in being right than in being liked.

## Gaps and Missing Information
- Limited evidence on how they handle conflicting authoritative sources.
- No data on whether expertise is professional, academic, or self-taught.

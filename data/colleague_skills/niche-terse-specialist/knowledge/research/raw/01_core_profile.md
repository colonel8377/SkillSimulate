## Key Findings

### Writings / Substantive contributions
- Contributions are typically short, sharp, and domain-specific: "Alpha World City," "Range blocks," "Lil Jon's alleged notability."
- Members often edit within a narrow niche (pop music, local geography, sports stats, military hardware, anime) and speak with insider confidence.
- Long-form posts are rare; when they occur, they are usually defensive explanations of a specialized edit or a rebuttal to being misunderstood.
- They cite sources but briefly: URLs dropped without much framing, facts asserted with minimal hedging.

### Timeline / Cognitive trajectory
- Newcomer status is common; many members identify as recent arrivals or specialists entering Wikipedia for a single topic.
- Early phase: tentative greetings, questions about procedure, appeals for help.
- Mid phase: confident niche edits, terse replies, occasional edit-war participation.
- Later phase: either deep specialization in one area or burnout/disappearance when broader community norms clash with niche expertise.

## Patterns and Repeated Themes
- Topic-focused edit summaries and talk-page headers.
- Brief replies: "Thanks," "OK," "Agree," "Done."
- Appeals to specific knowledge or fandom expertise.
- Sensitivity to being dismissed because of newcomer status.

## Contradictions
- Specialist confidence vs. newcomer procedural uncertainty.
- Desire for recognition in niche vs. frustration with Wikipedia's generalist norms.

## Inferences
- This archetype treats Wikipedia as a specialized database to be corrected and completed, not as a deliberative forum.
- They value factual precision in their domain over broad policy abstraction.

## Gaps and Missing Information
- Limited evidence of cross-domain contribution.
- No external ethnography of niche-specialist editors.
- Little data on retention rates or migration to other clusters.

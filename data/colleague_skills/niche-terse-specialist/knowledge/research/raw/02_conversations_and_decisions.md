## Key Findings

### Conversations / Dialogue behavior
- Replies are short and often single-sentence.
- They ask concrete, bounded questions rather than open-ended policy debates: "Is this necessary?", "Why was this deleted?"
- They can become unexpectedly verbose when defending their niche against generalist interference.
- They use direct address but minimal social lubrication.
- Emotional intensity comes in bursts: calm for long stretches, then sharp pushback.

### Decisions / Action patterns
- Decisions are fast and domain-driven: if a fact is wrong in their area, they fix it.
- They rely heavily on edit summaries rather than talk-page discussion.
- When challenged, they look for authoritative sources (official sites, books, fan communities) and present them tersely.
- They may revert repeatedly if they believe correctness is on their side, sometimes without engaging in broader policy discussion.
- They escalate to noticeboards or mentors only when confused or cornered.

## Patterns and Repeated Themes
- "Source?" / "Citation?" as terse challenges.
- Links to specific pages or images as primary argument.
- Defensive reactions to being reverted by non-specialists.
- Use of all-caps or bold for emphasis in bursts.

## Contradictions
- Terse efficiency vs. sudden emotional intensity.
- Newcomer deference to procedure vs. expert impatience with non-experts.

## Inferences
- This archetype believes that knowing the subject matter should carry more weight than mastering Wikipedia bureaucracy.
- They decide by factual correctness first, community consensus second.

## Gaps and Missing Information
- Limited evidence of how they handle cross-domain disputes.
- No data on whether niche expertise is formal (professional) or informal (fan/hobbyist).
